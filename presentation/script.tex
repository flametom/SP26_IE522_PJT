\slide{2}{Recap: Simulating Emergency Evacuation}
\begin{itemize}
\item Brief recap from our 1st presentation. The paper asks: when a hazard hits a community, how do thousands of pedestrians actually evacuate?
\item Each person decides independently on local information, panic causes irrational behavior, congestion is emergent --- a traditional process-flow model cannot capture this, so we need an agent-based simulation.
\end{itemize}

\slide{3}{Recap: Three-Component Model}
\begin{itemize}
\item Three coupled components from the paper: community network, human agents, hazard agents.
\item Two algorithms tie them together --- Algorithm 1 (human--network) and Algorithm 2 (human--hazard). Both run every step.
\end{itemize}

\slide{4}{Recap: Agent States \& Algorithms 1--2}
\begin{itemize}
\item Six states per human, with Algorithm 2 handling hazard exposure and Algorithm 1 handling routing.
\item Two points worth emphasizing: once impacted, destination flips to the nearest shelter; panic is irreversible.
\item We take both algorithms directly from the paper --- no changes to the decision logic.
\end{itemize}

\slide{5}{What we reproduced \& extended}
\begin{itemize}
\item Three reproduction targets from the paper: Phase 1 (five community networks), Phase 2a ($RI$ vs $P_{\max} \times H_{\max}$), Phase 2b ($RS/RC/RL$ vs panic).
\item Three extensions beyond the paper's range: wider $P_{\max}$, wider $H_{\max}$, finer panic grid.
\item The notation box at top defines every symbol used later.
\end{itemize}

\slide{6}{What the paper tells us --- and what it leaves to us}
\begin{itemize}
\item What the paper specifies about implementation: Python 3.7, Apple M1 Pro with 16 GB RAM, and measured runtime of 2.5 hours at Pmax=2K and 22 hours at Pmax=8K per single run. Algorithms 1 and 2 are decision logic only --- the paper does not say how paths are actually computed.
\item What the paper leaves silent: shortest-path library, per-step vs cached path policy, parallel execution, RNG design, reproducibility seeding. We had to pick a position on every one.
\item Key takeaway (bottom box): at the paper's 2.5 h/run, our 390-run experiment would take 41 days of sequential compute. Our pipeline does it in 3.4 hours. The engineering is the prerequisite for every chart in the rest of this deck.
\end{itemize}

\slide{7}{Gap A: Computing paths, 200K+ times per run}
\begin{itemize}
\item Algorithm 1 path recomputation is about 87\% of runtime: 2{,}000 agents across 121 steps is roughly 242K shortest-path queries per run. In pure Python per-agent, this is the paper's reported 2.5--22 hours.
\item Our fix: many agents share destinations (same buildings, same shelters), so compute one shortest-path tree per unique destination and let agents do an O(1) lookup. Shown in the mechanism diagram: per-agent queries on the left, shared destination trees on the right.
\item The bottom box is the Amdahl arithmetic: 87\% of runtime sped up by 230x gives 7.5x end-to-end. The other 13\% we left alone.
\end{itemize}

\slide{8}{Gap B: Running 390 experiments, honestly}
\begin{itemize}
\item Three more places the paper is silent, answered with one choice each. Per-step recomputation: the paper says pedestrians act on up-to-date observations, so we never cache paths. Parallel execution: the paper reports a single run, so the 8-worker multiprocessing layer is our addition --- without it, 390 runs at paper pace is 41 days. RNG streams: we split human / hazard / sim into three streams so that varying Pmax does not also vary the hazard scenario.
\item Bottom box: each row is a question the paper does not ask out loud. Answering them is what makes reproduction-at-scale possible rather than a single run.
\end{itemize}

\slide{9}{What this engineering unlocked}
\begin{itemize}
\item 10 seeds per config, all 5 communities validated in parallel, the 17x3 Pmax/Hmax grid for Ext 1, a 6-point Hmax sweep for Ext 2, a 9-point panic grid for Ext 3, three side-by-side comparison animations. Every one of these is finer than what the paper reported.
\item The gray caption at the bottom mentions the raw speedup --- 7.5x single-process, 60x overall --- but only as evidence. The headline is what the 3.4 hours bought us, not the multiplier on a benchmark.
\end{itemize}

\begin{itemize}

\slide{16}{Three questions we could ask}
\begin{itemize}
\item Three questions the paper's range didn't answer, which our faster pipeline lets us tackle.
\item Paper tested $P_{\max}$ up to 8K, $H_{\max}$ up to 5, panic at 5 points. We push each axis further in the next three slides.
\end{itemize}

\slide{17}{Extension 1: $P_{\max}$ breakpoint}
\begin{itemize}
\item Extended $P_{\max}$ from 2K to 97K across three $H_{\max}$ levels.
\item Independence holds up to 20K, then breaks at 50K by +8.5\%p.
\item Mechanism: 969 buildings at 20-person occupancy is about 20K capacity. At 50K we overflow --- agents queue outdoors and get hit by hazards.
\end{itemize}

\slide{18}{Extension 2: $H_{\max}$ saturation}
\begin{itemize}
\item Extended $H_{\max}$ from 5 to 30 at paper-baseline $P_{\max}$.
\item $RI$ saturates at about 86\% --- more hazards do not push past this ceiling.
\item Interpretation: likely about 14\% of the network is structurally unreachable by hazards.
\end{itemize}

\slide{19}{Extension 3: Finer panic grid}
\begin{itemize}
\item 9 panic points vs the paper's 5.
\item Curves are smooth, monotonic, with diminishing marginal effect --- no phase transitions between the paper's measured points.
\item Policy implication: panic management has highest payoff at low baseline panic.
\end{itemize}

\slide{23}{Summary}
\begin{itemize}
\item Reproduction successful within 2--4\%p of the paper across all three phases.
\item Three novel findings beyond the paper: $P_{\max}$ breakpoint at 50K, $H_{\max}$ saturation near 86\%, smooth diminishing panic effect.
\item The engineering enabled all of this without changing any paper algorithm --- the six-metric dashboard summarizes the scale.
\end{itemize}

\slide{24}{Limitations}
\begin{itemize}
\item Where we focused: faithful paper reproduction, an engineering improvement, and three extensions.
\item What we left on the table: no agent-model changes, no casualty-formula rewrite, no multi-community extension of Phase 2. The engineering-first stance was deliberate --- a fast pipeline is the prerequisite for any of the agent-side extensions.
\end{itemize}
