\slide{2}{Recap: Simulating Emergency Evacuation}
\begin{itemize}
\item Brief recap from our 1st presentation. The paper studies how thousands of pedestrians evacuate when a hazard affects a community.
\item The slide only lists the three main reasons this is hard: independent decisions, local information, and emergent panic/congestion. Say the fuller version verbally: each person chooses a destination and route, those choices depend on nearby roads and hazard exposure, and congestion is not imposed globally but emerges from many individual movements.
\item Therefore a traditional process-flow model is too aggregated. The paper uses agent-based simulation so each pedestrian has its own state, position, speed, destination, and decision logic.
\end{itemize}

\slide{3}{Recap: Three-Component Model}
\begin{itemize}
\item Three coupled components from the paper: community network, human agents, hazard agents.
\item Two algorithms tie them together --- Algorithm 1 (human--network) and Algorithm 2 (human--hazard). Both run every step.
\end{itemize}

\slide{4}{Recap: Agent States \& Algorithms 1--2}
\begin{itemize}
\item Six states per human, with Algorithm 2 handling hazard exposure and Algorithm 1 handling routing. The slide is intentionally compressed; give the operational details verbally.
\item Algorithm 2 runs every step: if an agent is inside a hazard for the first time, it becomes impacted and reroutes to a shelter; then casualty risk and panic are checked. Casualty probability is higher near the hazard center.
\item Algorithm 1 also runs every step: agents on edges check congestion and may queue; agents at nodes reroute, continue toward a destination, or choose a random route if panic has taken over.
\item Two points worth emphasizing: once impacted, destination flips to the nearest shelter; panic is irreversible.
\item We take both algorithms directly from the paper --- no changes to the decision logic.
\end{itemize}

\slide{5}{Reproduction and extension scope}
\begin{itemize}
\item Three reproduction targets from the paper: Phase 1 (five community networks), Phase 2a ($RI$ vs $P_{\max} \times H_{\max}$), Phase 2b ($RS/RC/RL$ vs panic).
\item If needed, expand the abbreviations verbally: $RI$ is impacted rate, $RS$ is survival rate, $RC$ is casualty rate, and $RL$ is leftover rate, meaning agents still stuck in hazard at the end.
\item Three extensions beyond the paper's range: wider $P_{\max}$, wider $H_{\max}$, finer panic grid.
\item The notation box at top defines every symbol used later.
\end{itemize}

\slide{6}{Implementation specifications and reproducibility gaps}
\begin{itemize}
\item The paper specifies the implementation environment, hardware, and measured runtime, but it does not fully specify the computational implementation. Algorithms 1 and 2 define decision logic, not the path-computation mechanism.
\item The main unspecified details are shortest-path implementation, per-step versus cached path policy, parallel execution, RNG design, and reproducibility seeding. These choices materially affect whether a multi-seed reproduction is feasible.
\item Key takeaway: at the paper's 2.5 h/run baseline, our 390-run core grid would require about 41 days of sequential compute. The optimized pipeline completes that grid in 3.4 hours, making the subsequent reproduction and extension analyses practical.
\end{itemize}

\slide{7}{Computational bottleneck: repeated path computation}
\begin{itemize}
\item Algorithm 1 path recomputation dominates runtime: 2{,}000 agents across 121 steps is roughly 242K shortest-path queries per run.
\item Optimization: many agents share destinations, so instead of asking the graph for a new path for every agent, we group by destination, build one predecessor tree for each destination, and reconstruct each agent's path from that tree.
\item The lower summary box reports the measured runtime change: the routing subsystem drops from 165.9 seconds to about 20.0 seconds including tree construction, and the full run drops from 168 seconds to 22.5 seconds.
\end{itemize}

\slide{8}{Experimental design for scalable reproduction}
\begin{itemize}
\item Three implementation decisions support scalable reproduction: recompute paths every step, execute the grid with 8 workers, and vary agent seeds while keeping the hazard seed fixed.
\item These are implementation choices, not new behavioral rules. They preserve the original model logic while making multi-seed reproduction possible.
\end{itemize}

\slide{9}{Experimental scale enabled by the implementation}
\begin{itemize}
\item The optimized implementation supports four analysis components: 10 seeds per configuration, a 390-run core grid, a 510-run Pmax sweep, and three visual comparison animations.
\item The gray caption reports the measured speedup: 7.5x single-process and about 60x with 8 workers.
\end{itemize}

\slide{13}{Phase 2a: $RI$ vs $P_{\max} \times H_{\max}$}
\begin{itemize}
\item Fig.\ 5 reproduction. Our qualitative trend matches: $RI$ increases with $H_{\max}$ and is nearly independent of $P_{\max}$ in the paper range.
\item Critical confirmation: $RI$ is independent of $P_{\max}$ from 2K to 8K (delta under 0.2\%p). This is the paper's core finding, and we reproduced it.
\item Quantitative match is partial, not exact: mean absolute error is about 5 percentage points and the largest gap is about 9 percentage points.
\end{itemize}

\slide{14}{Phase 2b: $RS / RC / RL$ vs panic}
\begin{itemize}
\item Fig.\ 6 reproduction using the paper's five panic points. All three directions match: higher panic lowers survival and raises casualty and leftover.
\item Quantitative levels are only partially matched. The largest visible gap is high-panic casualty: our implementation levels off near 6\%, while the paper reaches about 13\%.
\item Therefore the academically precise interpretation is qualitative reproduction with remaining model gaps, rather than exact numerical reproduction.
\end{itemize}

\slide{15}{Calibration choices and remaining gaps}
\begin{itemize}
\item The slide is intentionally simpler than the notes: the three main unpublished inputs are shelter locations, casualty formula, and hazard seed. We calibrated each once, then held it fixed for the rest of the deck.
\item Shelter locations matter most for survival. The table shows why we use about 600 shelters: it gives 64.3\% survival at panic = 90\%, closest to the paper's 64.6\%.
\item The remaining gaps should be stated explicitly: $RI$ is a little high overall, and high-panic casualty remains low because our casualty formula is conservative relative to the paper's implied behavior.
\end{itemize}

\slide{16}{Extension study design beyond the paper range}
\begin{itemize}
\item The optimized pipeline supports three controlled extensions beyond the paper range.
\item The paper tested $P_{\max}$ up to 8K, $H_{\max}$ up to 15, and panic at five points. We extend each axis in the next three slides.
\end{itemize}

\slide{17}{Extension 1: $P_{\max}$ threshold}
\begin{itemize}
\item Extended $P_{\max}$ from 2K to 97K across three $H_{\max}$ levels.
\item Independence holds up to 20K, and a measurable deviation appears at 50K by +8.5\%p.
\item Mechanism hypothesis: at 50K, building-to-network connectors and nearby walk edges become bottlenecks, so more agents queue outdoors and remain exposed longer.
\end{itemize}

\slide{18}{Extension 2: $H_{\max}$ saturation}
\begin{itemize}
\item Extended $H_{\max}$ from 5 to 30 at paper-baseline $P_{\max}$.
\item $RI$ approaches an empirical plateau near 86\% by $H_{\max}=30$.
\item Interpretation: under this hazard-generation process, the remaining 14\% appears to be low-exposure or peripheral origins, not a universal ceiling.
\end{itemize}

\slide{19}{Extension 3: Finer panic grid}
\begin{itemize}
\item 9 panic points vs the paper's 5.
\item Curves are smooth and monotonic at 10\% resolution; no abrupt transition is visible in this calibrated scenario.
\item Model implication: marginal panic reduction has the largest effect at low baseline panic.
\end{itemize}
